## MEDICAL EMERGENCIES

### Bleeding Control
1. Apply direct pressure with clean cloth
2. Elevate wound above heart if possible
3. If bleeding through, add more cloth — don't remove first layer
4. For severe limb bleeding: tourniquet 2-3 inches above wound, note time applied
5. Tourniquets: tighten until bleeding stops, max 2 hours before tissue damage
6. Wound packing: for deep wounds, pack clean cloth firmly into wound, then apply pressure

### CPR (Adult)
1. Check responsiveness — tap shoulders, shout
2. Call for help
3. 30 chest compressions: heel of hand on center of chest, 2 inches deep, 100-120/min
4. 2 rescue breaths: tilt head back, lift chin, pinch nose, breathe until chest rises
5. Continue 30:2 ratio until help arrives or person recovers

CPR (Child 1-8 years): same ratio, one hand, 1.5 inches deep
CPR (Infant <1 year): 2 fingers on sternum, 1.5 inches, 30:2, cover mouth AND nose with your mouth

### Choking (Adult)
1. If coughing, encourage continued coughing
2. If cannot breathe/speak: stand behind, make fist above navel
3. Grasp fist with other hand, thrust inward and upward
4. Repeat until object expelled or person unconscious
5. If unconscious: begin CPR, check mouth for object before breaths

Choking (Infant): 5 back blows (face down on forearm), then 5 chest thrusts (face up, 2 fingers on sternum). Alternate until clear.

### Shock
Signs: pale/cool/clammy skin, rapid weak pulse, rapid shallow breathing, confusion
Treatment:
1. Lay person flat, elevate legs 12 inches (unless head/spine injury)
2. Keep warm with blankets
3. Don't give food or water
4. Monitor breathing

### Burns
- Minor (red, no blisters): cool running water 10-20 min, aloe vera, loose bandage
- Moderate (blisters): cool water, don't pop blisters, loose sterile bandage
- Severe (white/charred): don't remove clothing stuck to burn, cover loosely, seek help
- Chemical burns: flush with water 20+ minutes, remove contaminated clothing
- Electrical burns: ensure power off before touching person, check for exit wound

Burns rule of 9s (adult body surface area):
- Head: 9% | Each arm: 9% | Chest: 18% | Back: 18% | Each leg: 18% | Groin: 1%
- Burns >15% BSA in adults or >10% in children = critical, risk of shock

### Wound Closure
When to close: clean cuts <6 hours old, not from bites, no signs of infection
When NOT to close: puncture wounds, animal/human bites, dirty wounds, wounds >12 hours old

Butterfly strips / steri-strips:
1. Clean wound thoroughly with clean water
2. Dry skin around wound (not inside)
3. Apply strips perpendicular to wound, pulling edges together
4. Space strips 3mm apart, leave gaps for drainage
5. Apply a strip lengthwise over the ends to anchor

Improvised sutures (last resort, only for gaping wounds that won't stay closed):
1. Sterilize needle (boil 20 min or hold in flame until red)
2. Use thin, strong thread (fishing line, dental floss) — boil first
3. Enter skin 5mm from wound edge, exit same distance on other side
4. Tie square knots, snug but not tight (skin swells)
5. Space sutures 5-7mm apart
6. Watch for infection: increasing redness, warmth, pus, red streaks, fever

### Fractures/Sprains
1. Immobilize the injury — splint in position found
2. Ice wrapped in cloth — 20 min on, 20 min off
3. Elevate if possible
4. For open fracture (bone visible): cover wound, don't push bone back, splint around it

Splinting: immobilize joint above AND below the fracture. Pad all bony points. Check circulation below splint (pulse, color, warmth) every 15 min.

### Head Injury
Concussion signs: headache, confusion, nausea, dizziness, brief unconsciousness
DANGER signs (seek urgent help):
- Unequal pupils
- Clear fluid from nose or ears
- Worsening headache
- Repeated vomiting
- Seizures
- Cannot be awakened
- Weakness on one side

Keep awake and monitor for 24 hours. Wake every 2 hours if sleeping.

### Heat Emergencies
Heat exhaustion: heavy sweating, weakness, cool/pale skin, nausea
- Move to shade, cool water, fan, sip water

Heat stroke: hot/red/dry skin, high temp (>104°F/40°C), confusion, unconscious
- EMERGENCY — cool rapidly: ice packs to neck/armpits/groin, cool water immersion
- Do NOT give fluids if unconscious

### Hypothermia
Signs: shivering, confusion, slurred speech, drowsiness
Severe: shivering stops, very slow pulse, unconsciousness
1. Move to warmth/shelter
2. Remove wet clothing
3. Warm center of body first (chest, neck, head, groin)
4. Warm beverages if conscious (no alcohol, no caffeine)
5. Skin-to-skin contact under blankets if available
6. Handle gently — rough movement can cause cardiac arrest in severe hypothermia

### Dehydration
Signs: thirst, dark urine, dry mouth, headache, dizziness
Severe: no urine, sunken eyes, rapid pulse, confusion

Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS):
- Recipe: 1 liter clean water + 6 level teaspoons sugar + ½ level teaspoon salt
- Give small sips every 1-2 minutes
- For children: 1 cup (250ml) after each loose stool
- Continue even if vomiting — give smaller amounts more frequently

### Allergic Reaction (Anaphylaxis)
Signs: swelling face/throat, difficulty breathing, hives, rapid pulse, dizziness
1. EpiPen if available: outer thigh, through clothing, hold 10 seconds
2. Lay flat with legs elevated (or sitting if breathing difficulty)
3. Second EpiPen after 5-15 min if no improvement
4. If no EpiPen: antihistamine (diphenhydramine/Benadryl) — won't stop severe reaction but helps

### Poisoning
1. Identify what was ingested if possible
2. Do NOT induce vomiting (especially for corrosives, petroleum products)
3. If conscious: activated charcoal (1g per kg body weight) mixed in water, within 1 hour of ingestion
4. If on skin: remove contaminated clothing, flush with water 15-20 min
5. If inhaled: move to fresh air

### Dental Emergencies
Knocked-out tooth:
1. Handle by crown only (never touch root)
2. Rinse gently with clean water — do not scrub
3. Reimplant into socket if possible, bite on cloth to hold
4. If can't reimplant: store in milk, saliva, or saline
5. Reimplant within 30 min for best chance of survival

Toothache/abscess:
- Rinse with warm salt water (1 tsp salt per cup)
- Ibuprofen for pain (see dosage table below)
- Clove oil on cotton ball applied to tooth (natural anesthetic — eugenol)
- Abscess (swelling with pus): warm salt water rinses every 2 hours. If fever or spreading redness, antibiotics needed

Broken tooth: cover sharp edge with dental wax, chewing gum, or candle wax to protect tongue

### Drowning
1. Remove from water (protect your own safety first)
2. If not breathing: begin CPR immediately
3. Do NOT attempt to drain water from lungs (Heimlich doesn't help)
4. Continue CPR for at least 30 minutes — cold water drowning victims can recover after long submersion
5. Even if person seems fine after rescue: monitor for 24 hours (secondary drowning from water in lungs)

### Seizures
1. Protect from injury — clear area of hard/sharp objects
2. Do NOT put anything in mouth
3. Do NOT restrain
4. Turn on side after seizure stops (recovery position)
5. Time the seizure — if >5 minutes, this is an emergency

## COMMON MEDICATION DOSAGES

IMPORTANT: These are general guidelines for common over-the-counter medications. When in doubt, use the lowest effective dose. Adjust for elderly (use lower doses) and those with liver/kidney problems.

### Pain & Fever

Ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin):
- Adult: 200-400mg every 4-6 hours, max 1200mg/day (OTC) or 3200mg (prescribed)
- Child 6mo-12yr: 5-10mg per kg every 6-8 hours
- Take with food. Avoid if kidney problems, stomach ulcers, or heavy bleeding.

Acetaminophen / Paracetamol (Tylenol):
- Adult: 500-1000mg every 4-6 hours, max 3000mg/day
- Child: 10-15mg per kg every 4-6 hours
- DANGER: Overdose causes liver failure. Never exceed max dose. Avoid with alcohol.

Aspirin:
- Adult: 325-650mg every 4 hours, max 4000mg/day
- Heart attack: chew 1 regular aspirin (325mg) immediately
- NEVER give to children <16 (risk of Reye's syndrome)

### Allergies

Diphenhydramine (Benadryl):
- Adult: 25-50mg every 4-6 hours, max 300mg/day
- Child 6-12: 12.5-25mg every 4-6 hours
- Causes drowsiness. Use for allergic reactions, itching, insomnia.

Loratadine (Claritin) / Cetirizine (Zyrtec):
- Adult: 10mg once daily
- Child 2-6: 5mg once daily
- Non-drowsy antihistamines. For ongoing allergies, not acute reactions.

### Diarrhea

Loperamide (Imodium):
- Adult: 4mg first dose, then 2mg after each loose stool, max 16mg/day
- Do NOT use if bloody stool or fever >101°F/38.5°C (may be dysentery — body needs to expel infection)

### Stomach

Antacids (Tums, calcium carbonate):
- Adult: 500-1000mg as needed, max 7500mg/day
- For heartburn, indigestion, calcium supplement

### Topical

Antibiotic ointment (Neosporin, bacitracin):
- Apply thin layer to clean wounds 1-3x daily
- Cover with clean bandage
- Stop if rash develops (allergic reaction)

Hydrocortisone cream (1%):
- For itching, rashes, insect bites, mild eczema
- Apply thin layer 2-4x daily, max 7 days
- Do NOT use on open wounds or infected skin

## INFECTION RECOGNITION

Signs of wound infection (usually 2-5 days after injury):
1. Increasing redness spreading from wound
2. Increasing pain and swelling
3. Warmth around wound
4. Pus or cloudy drainage
5. Red streaks extending from wound (lymphangitis — URGENT)
6. Fever, chills, body aches

If red streaks, high fever, or rapidly spreading redness: this is a life-threatening emergency. Antibiotics are critical. Draw a line around the redness edge with pen and monitor hourly for spread.

## SURVIVAL PRIORITIES

### Rule of 3s
- 3 minutes without air
- 3 hours without shelter (extreme conditions)
- 3 days without water
- 3 weeks without food

### Water
Finding:
- Follow animal trails downhill (often lead to water)
- Birds flying low at dawn/dusk often heading to water
- Dig in dry riverbeds, outside bends
- Collect dew with cloth at dawn
- Rain collection
- Green vegetation in arid areas indicates water nearby

Purification (priority order):
1. Boiling: rolling boil for 1 minute (3 min above 6500 ft / 2000m)
2. Chemical: 2 drops household bleach (5-6% sodium hypochlorite) per liter, wait 30 min (double if cloudy)
3. Iodine: 5 drops 2% tincture per liter clear water, wait 30 min
4. Solar: clear PET bottle in direct sun 6+ hours (SODIS method)
5. Filter through cloth, sand, charcoal (removes particles, not all pathogens)

Daily water needs: 2-3 liters minimum. More in heat, at altitude, or with physical exertion. Signs of dehydration begin at 1-2% body water loss.

### Fire
Tinder (catches spark): dry grass, bark shavings, dryer lint, birch bark, fatwood, char cloth
Kindling (builds flame): small dry twigs, pencil thickness
Fuel (sustains): larger dry wood, wrist to arm thickness

Fire lay methods:
- Teepee: tinder in center, kindling leaned around it
- Log cabin: tinder in center, kindling stacked around
- Lean-to: tinder against log, kindling leaned on log
- Dakota fire hole: two connected holes — burns hot with less smoke, less visible

Fire starting:
- Matches/lighter (keep dry in sealed bag)
- Ferro rod: scrape with knife spine at 45°
- Bow drill: hardwood fireboard, softwood spindle, fast bow strokes
- Hand drill: softwood fireboard, straight dry spindle, roll between palms
- Battery + steel wool: touch terminals to fine steel wool, it ignites instantly
- Flint + steel: strike at sharp angle, catch sparks on char cloth

### Shelter
Priorities: protection from wind, rain, ground cold
Location: avoid hilltops (wind), valleys (cold air pools), dead trees, flood zones
Types:
- Debris hut: A-frame of branch, pile leaves/debris 3 ft thick
- Lean-to: angled roof against support, debris covering
- Snow cave: dig into drift, sleeping platform higher than entrance
- Tarp/poncho shelter: fastest option if materials available

Insulation from ground is critical — use leaves, pine needles, bark. Ground steals body heat 20x faster than air.

### Food
Priorities: water and shelter come first, you can survive weeks without food

Safe to eat (when properly identified):
- Dandelions (all parts)
- Cattails (roots, shoots, pollen)
- Acorns (leach tannins by soaking in running water or boiling with water changes)
- Pine needles (tea = vitamin C; inner bark is edible)
- Clover (flowers and leaves)
- Plantain (the weed, not the banana — leaves are edible and medicinal)

Universal Edibility Test (last resort — takes 24+ hours):
1. Test one plant part at a time
2. Smell — reject if bitter/soapy
3. Touch to wrist — wait 15 min for reaction
4. Touch to lip — wait 15 min
5. Touch to tongue — wait 15 min
6. Chew small amount, spit out — wait 15 min
7. Swallow small amount — wait 8 hours
8. Eat larger amount — wait 8 hours
9. If no reaction, plant part is likely safe

AVOID: mushrooms (too risky without expert ID), plants with milky sap (except dandelion), umbrella-shaped flower clusters, shiny leaves, plants with almond scent in leaves/bark, white or yellow berries

### Trapping & Fishing
Simple snare: loop of wire or cord on an animal trail, anchored to a stake or tree
Fishing without a rod: gorge hook (sharpened stick with bait), hand lines, improvised net
Insects: grasshoppers, crickets, ants, larvae — remove legs/wings, cook if possible. Avoid brightly colored insects.

### Navigation
Without compass:
- Sun rises in east, sets in west (roughly)
- In Northern Hemisphere: sun is due south at solar noon
- North Star (Polaris): follow Big Dipper's two "pointer stars" 5x the distance between them
- Stick shadow method: mark shadow tip, wait 15 min, mark again — line points east-west
- Crescent moon: line through the horns points roughly south (Northern Hemisphere)
- Moss grows on all sides of trees (unreliable as sole method)

With map:
- Orient map to terrain features
- Triangulate position using 3 known landmarks
- Follow terrain features (ridges, rivers) rather than trying to hold a bearing through dense forest

### Signaling
- Signal fires: 3 fires in triangle (international distress)
- Green branches/leaves on fire for smoke during day
- Mirror flash toward aircraft (any shiny surface works)
- Ground signals: large X = need medical help; V = need assistance; → = traveling this direction
- Bright clothing/materials in open areas
- Whistle: 3 blasts = distress (carry a whistle — sound travels farther than voice)

### Knots
- Bowline: fixed loop that won't slip (rescue, tie to anchor)
- Clove hitch: quick attachment to a post or tree (shelters, securing loads)
- Taut-line hitch: adjustable tension (tent/tarp guy lines)
- Figure-8: stopper knot, also used in climbing
- Square knot: joining two ropes of equal diameter (bandaging, bundles)
